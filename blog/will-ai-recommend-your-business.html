Will AI Recommend Your Business? What Generative Search Means for Getting Found

AI Search Business Growth Getting Found

Something quiet has changed in how people find businesses. More and more of your potential customers no longer open Google and scroll a page of links. They ask an AI — ChatGPT, Claude, or Perplexity — "who's a good company for this?" and read the short answer it gives back. Which raises one uncomfortable question for every business owner: when an AI is asked to recommend someone in your line of work, is it recommending you?

Search is becoming an answer, not a list

For twenty years, being found meant ranking on the first page of Google — one of ten blue links a person might click. It was a competition for attention, but at least there were ten slots and the customer did the choosing.

AI search collapses that. Ask an assistant for a recommendation and you rarely get ten options — you get one answer, or a short list of two or three names, phrased with confidence. The customer often doesn't scroll, doesn't compare, doesn't click through to a page of results. They take the answer. If your business isn't in it, you weren't rejected; you were simply never mentioned. And you'll never see it happen, because there's no click to show up in your analytics.

Why this isn't the SEO you already know

The instinct is to treat this like search-engine optimisation with a new coat of paint. It isn't, and the difference matters for where you spend effort.

  • You can't buy your way in. There are no ads in an AI's recommendation. It answers from what it has learned about the world, not from who paid the most.
  • It rewards being understood, not being loud. Keyword tricks that once gamed Google don't move an AI. What moves it is whether it clearly understands what you do, who you serve, and why you're credible.
  • It runs on your whole reputation, not one page. An AI forms its answer from everything it has seen about you across the web — your site, your profiles, mentions of you elsewhere — woven into a single picture. One polished landing page isn't enough if the rest of your presence is thin or contradictory.

What actually decides whether an AI recommends you

Strip away the jargon and it comes down to four business questions you can answer honestly about your own presence today.

1. Clarity — does the AI understand what you do?

If a smart human landed on your website and couldn't say, in one sentence, exactly what you offer and who it's for, an AI can't either. Vague, we-do-everything positioning is invisible to AI, because it can't confidently match you to a specific request.

2. Consistency — do you tell the same story everywhere?

Your website, your LinkedIn, your listings, your profiles — if they describe your business differently, the AI can't merge them into one confident picture of who you are. When the story is consistent across every place you appear, the AI trusts it and repeats it.

3. Credibility — does anyone else vouch for you?

Being mentioned elsewhere — articles, directories, other people's content, genuine reviews — is what turns "a business that claims to be good" into "a business the wider web treats as real and reputable." AI weighs those outside signals heavily, because they're harder to fake than your own marketing.

4. Legibility — is your information machine-readable?

There's a technical layer too: whether your site presents its facts in a structured way that machines can read cleanly, and whether it even allows AI systems to access it. This is the part most businesses get wrong by accident — and the part where a little expert help goes a long way. This isn't theory — it already happened to me

A company that sells an AI product needed to find people who'd be a good fit for it. Instead of buying an ad list, they used an AI-powered search tool to identify relevant people automatically — and it surfaced me and prompted them to reach out. Not because I ran a campaign, but because my presence was clear and legible enough for an AI to understand who I was and decide I was relevant. That's this whole shift in one small story: the machine did the finding, and being understandable to it was the entire game. I broke down the mechanics in how AI search found me.

What to do about it — without touching code

You don't need to become technical to take the first steps. In order of impact:

  • Sharpen your positioning. Make it obvious, in plain words, what you do and who you do it for. Specific beats broad every time.
  • Make your story consistent. Align how your business is described across your site and every profile and listing, so they reinforce one clear picture.
  • Earn honest mentions. Guest articles, genuine reviews, being listed where your industry is discussed — each one is a vote the AI can see.
  • Get the technical foundation right. Structured, machine-readable facts and AI-crawler access are where most sites quietly lose — and where it's worth getting an expert to look, because it's invisible from the outside and easy to get wrong.

The bottom line

A growing share of your future customers will meet your business for the first time inside an AI's answer, not on a search results page. Being the name that answer contains isn't luck — it's the result of being clear, consistent, credible, and legible to machines. The businesses that treat this as seriously as they once treated Google will own the recommendation. The ones that ignore it will slowly, invisibly disappear from the conversation.
